Basic Rules of Armwrestling

The Foundation of Fair Competition

Basic rules form the foundation of all armwrestling competitions. These rules ensure fair play, prevent injuries, and create a standardized competition format. Every competitor must understand and follow these basic rules to be allowed to compete.

Official Grip Requirements

Palm-to-Palm Contact: Your hand must be palm-down on your opponent's hand with palm-to-palm contact. Fingers can be gripped, but the palm must make contact with the opponent's palm.

Thumb Position: Your thumb must not be placed on top of your opponent's hand. It must wrap around their hand with your other fingers.

Finger Placement: Your fingers wrap firmly around your opponent's hand. No fingers can be placed on the back of the opponent's hand—they must wrap around the palm side.

No Slipping: Once the match begins, your hand position is locked. You cannot adjust your grip once action starts.

Hand and Arm Positioning

Elbow on Pad: Your elbow must remain on the armwrestling pad throughout the match. Lifting your elbow results in immediate loss.

Shoulder Position: Your shoulder must be positioned at a 90-degree angle to the table. You cannot lean forward or backward excessively.

Upper Arm Contact: Your upper arm above the elbow should make contact with the pad. The entire arm must remain on the pad.

Body Position: You must be seated or standing properly at the table. You cannot use your body weight to lean into the match excessively.

Legal Moves & Actions

  • Hook Technique: Curling your wrist and forearm downward is legal as long as your elbow stays on the pad.
  • Toproll Technique: Rolling your arm over your opponent's arm is legal.
  • Side Pressure: Applying lateral pressure is legal as long as you maintain proper positioning.
  • Pressure Techniques: Applying maximum hand and finger pressure is legal.
  • Arm Rotation: Rotating your arm and hand is legal in most positions.

Common Fouls & Penalties

  • Elbow Off Pad: If your elbow leaves the pad, you lose immediately. This is the most common foul.
  • Grip Break: Breaking your grip during the match results in a foul and loss.
  • Shoulder Foul: Improper shoulder positioning can result in a warning or penalty.
  • False Start: Moving before the referee signals "go" results in a false start warning.
  • Wrist Grip Foul: Certain wrist positions can be considered illegal depending on the federation.

Winning Conditions

Pin to Pad: The primary way to win is to pin your opponent's hand to the pad. Once their hand touches the pad, the match is over and you win.

Opponent Submission: If your opponent submits or signals that they surrender, you win immediately.

Opponent Foul: If your opponent commits a foul such as lifting their elbow, you win by foul.

Time Limit: If neither competitor achieves a pin within the time limit, the match is judged by judges based on positioning and control.
